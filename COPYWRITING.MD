## 0. HOW TO USE THIS FILE — THE RMBC OPERATING ORDER

This playbook runs on **Stefan Georgi's RMBC Method: Research → Mechanism → Brief → Copy.** RMBC is the master framework — it front-loads 100% of the thinking so that, by the time you write, the copy is fill-in-the-blanks instead of staring at a blank page. **Never start writing prose first.** Work the four phases strictly in order (the full system is Section 22):

- **R — RESEARCH (≈50% of the entire job).** Mine the product, the avatar's exact words (Voice of Customer), the market/competition, and the proof. The copy is won or lost here. → Section 22.1
- **M — MECHANISM.** From the research, lock the Unique Mechanism of the **PROBLEM** (the hidden root cause) and of the **SOLUTION** (the new lever your product pulls). → Sections 22.2 + 7
- **B — BRIEF.** Distill research + mechanism into a one-document battle plan (Big Idea, claims, proof, emotion, objections, offer). Engineer the offer here with the Grand Slam Offer Engine (Section 21). → Sections 22.3 + 1
- **C — COPY.** Only now write — a fast "vomit draft" against the Skeleton (Section 3), pulling rhythm and lines from the Swipe Bank (Section 19), then edit in hard passes. → Sections 22.4 + 3 / 5 / 9 / 10

Then run the **"Gun-to-the-Head" QA pass** (Section 20) and rewrite anything that fails.

**The golden rule of this entire file:** *Specificity, emotion, and curiosity beat cleverness every single time.* If a sentence is not advancing the story, deepening an emotion, proving a claim, or opening/closing a curiosity loop — cut it.

**Why the order is non-negotiable:** writers fail when they write before they know. Research supplies the avatar's words, the pains, the objections, and the proof; Mechanism supplies the "why"; the Brief makes every decision in advance. Skip a phase and you'll waffle, generalize, and stall in the draft. Do them in order and the Copy phase is mostly assembly.

### 0.1 THE NON-NEGOTIABLE WRITING RULES (HOUSE STYLE)

These rules govern every word of copy you deliver. They beat any other guidance in this file if the two ever clash. If a draft breaks even one of them, the draft is not finished.

*(This block is written in the exact style it asks for: no em dashes, plain words, short complete lines.)*

1. **No em dashes. Ever.** Use a period, a comma, or a new line instead. This applies to all copy you hand over.
2. **Always finish with the humanizer skill.** Run the full draft through the humanizer skill before you deliver it. It strips out AI tells and stiff phrasing. This is a required final step, not an optional one. Reach for humanize-writing too when the voice still feels off.
3. **No sentences that stop abruptly.** A sentence can be short, but it has to feel whole and lead softly into the next one. No clipped, robotic fragments that jolt the reader. This replaces the old "fragments for punch" idea in Section 10.3.
4. **Write the way people actually talk.** Plain, spoken, simple. If you would not say it out loud to a friend across a table, do not write it.
5. **Never try to be clever.** Clever serves the writer's ego. Clear serves the reader. No wordplay, no showing off, no cute lines. Clear wins every time.
6. **No words a normal person would never say.** Cut "utilize," "leverage," "furthermore," "moreover," "delve," "myriad," "plethora," and anything that smells like a thesaurus. Pick the small, everyday word instead.
7. **Simple enough for a 6-year-old.** Short, common words. One idea at a time. If a child would get lost, rewrite the line.
8. **Push every word and sentence to the absolute truth.** Question each one until it is true and you can prove it. No fluff, no hype you cannot back up, no stretching the facts. If it is not true, it does not go in.
9. **Every sentence must answer "So what?"** Read each line and ask what it really means for the reader. If the line does not earn its place, cut it.
10. **Lots of white space.** No walls of text. Big blocks scare readers off and are hard to follow on a screen.
11. **One to three lines per paragraph, at most.** Each one has to make full sense on its own. It should never feel cut off, padded, or forced.
12. **Transitions have to feel natural.** One thought should flow into the next like a real conversation, never bolted on.
13. **Highlighted lines must connect both ways.** Anything you put on a coloured or bold background to drive a point has to grow out of the sentence before it and lead into the sentence after it. It can never sit there alone.
14. **Every word connects to the next.** The whole piece should read as one smooth, unbroken chain. Each sentence pulls the reader straight into the next one.

**Final move before delivery:** read the whole thing out loud, top to bottom, then run the humanizer skill. If any line makes you stumble, or a 6-year-old would not follow it, fix it and read it again.

## 1. THE PRE-WRITING BRIEF (RMBC's "B" — fill this after Research + Mechanism)

This is the RMBC Brief (see Section 22.3). **Do not fill it from imagination** — fill it from the Research doc (22.1) and the locked Mechanism (22.2). Every line below should be backed by a real quote, fact, or study you gathered. Answer all of these in one or two lines each before writing a word:

- **Product:** What is literally being sold? (pill, tea, program, newsletter, software)
- **Avatar (one person):** Age, gender, life stage, what their day feels like. Write to ONE human, never "people."
- **The pain (surface):** What they'd say out loud at the doctor / to a friend.
- **The pain (deep / emotional):** Shame, fear, loss of identity, letting loved ones down. *This is what you actually sell against.*
- **The dream (destination):** The specific scene of their life after the product works.
- **Market Awareness** (see 2.3): Unaware / Problem-aware / Solution-aware / Product-aware / Most-aware.
- **Market Sophistication** (see 2.4): Are they hearing claim #1, or claim #500? (Determines how hard you must twist the mechanism.)
- **The Villain:** Who/what is to blame for their pain (and is NOT their fault)?
- **The Big Idea:** The single, fresh, fascinating concept the whole piece hangs on (Section 2.1).
- **The Unique Mechanism — PROBLEM:** The "real hidden reason" nothing has worked (Section 7).
- **The Unique Mechanism — SOLUTION:** The new lever your product pulls that nothing else does.
- **Dominant Emotion:** Pick ONE primary emotion to ride (fear, hope, anger, shame→relief, pride, greed, belonging).
- **The Offer (Grand Slam — see Section 21):** Core + stacked/named bonuses + premium price + anchor + risk-reversing guarantee + scarcity/urgency. Score it on the **Value Equation**: is the Dream Outcome huge, the Likelihood believable, the Time Delay short, and the Effort/Sacrifice near zero?
- **The One Thing:** If the reader remembers only one sentence, what is it?

## 2. CORE PHILOSOPHY

### 2.1 The Big Idea
Every winning control is built on ONE big, fascinating idea — not a list of benefits. It is usually expressible in a single intriguing sentence:
- *"A long-forgotten Costa Rican tea ritual melts fat while you sleep."*
- *"Nerve pain leaves a 'fingerprint' — and one enzyme is the culprit."* (Nerve Control 911)
- *"A new pill is a 'time capsule in a bottle' that rewinds aging."* (Stem Cell Restore)
- *"CRISPR lets scientists cut-and-paste disease out of your body — and one tiny company owns the patent."* (Cut & Paste)
- *"Your body has two settings — Growth Mode and Survival Mode — and a switch flips it."* (IM-21)

If you cannot say the idea in one sentence that makes someone go *"wait, what?"*, you don't have copy yet. Go back.

### 2.2 Enter the conversation already in their head
Robert Collier's law. You don't start a new conversation — you join the one already running in the reader's mind. The Tyson letter opens mid-thought: *"I've always felt like a fat, scared little kid."* The reader who feels that way is instantly hooked.

### 2.3 Market Awareness (Eugene Schwartz) — pick the right entry point
- **Unaware** → lead with a story or a startling fact. (Costa Rican tea story; "Did you ever bury a time capsule as a kid?")
- **Problem-aware** → lead with the problem dramatized, then reveal the hidden cause. (Nerve Control 911, Feel Good Knees)
- **Solution-aware** → lead with the *mechanism* and why it's different. (CRISPR, IM-21)
- **Product-aware** → lead with the offer, proof, and a reason to act now.
- **Most-aware** → just the deal + urgency.

### 2.4 Market Sophistication — out-claim the claims
If your market has heard "lose weight" 500 times, you cannot say "lose weight." You must **wrap the claim in a new mechanism**: not "burn fat," but "regenerate your mitochondria with a Costa Rican tea." The mechanism is what makes an old promise believable again. **The more sophisticated the market, the more the mechanism does the heavy lifting.**

### 2.5 Sell the destination, not the product
Nobody wants tea, pills, or a newsletter. They want to button their pants, play with grandkids without losing breath, stop being ashamed naked in front of their spouse, retire rich, walk without a limp. Always sell the *scene of the transformed life*.

### 2.6 One emotion, ridden hard
Pick a dominant emotion and sustain it. Tyson = shame → reclaimed pride. Nerve Control 911 = fear/love (a son watching his mother rot). TradeSmith = financial fear → hope. Don't scatter; ride one wave.

## 4. HEADLINES, PRE-HEADS & DECKS

### 4.1 Anatomy
- **Pre-head (eyebrow):** small line above the headline that targets/qualifies or teases. *"An urgent message for anyone who's worried about their portfolio:"* (TradeSmith) · *"Mike Tyson confessional bombshell…"* (IM-21) · *"It's a $75 billion market." — Investors Business Daily* (Cut & Paste).
- **Headline:** the biggest promise + curiosity. Big benefit, fresh mechanism, or shocking proclamation.
- **Deck/subhead:** expands the promise and stacks secondary benefits + curiosity bullets.

### 4.2 Proven headline formulas (with live examples)
1. **How [unexpected source] [achieved big result] without [pain]:**
   *"How a Long-Forgotten Costa Rican Tradition Helped Me Melt 167 Lbs of Stubborn Fat in Under 1 Year WITHOUT Diet or Exercise!"*
2. **Secret/Discovery:** *"How an accidental discovery unlocks a secret weapon against aging."* (Shallenberger)
3. **Proclamation / contrarian:** *"Investing Is DEAD!"* (TradeSmith) · *"Knee Replacement Surgery… Obsolete."* (Feel Good Knees)
4. **"Hidden trigger / fingerprint" curiosity:** *"'Hidden Trigger' For Nerve Pain Discovered In An Episode Of CSI…"*
5. **Metaphor headline ("Like a ___ in a ___"):** *"Like a Time Capsule In a Bottle…"* (Stem Cell)
6. **News + authority stack:** *"New Breakthrough Can 'Cut & Paste' Disease From Your Body."*
7. **Time + specificity:** *"This 1,000-Year-Old 'Healing Exercise' Can Help Erase Knee Pain In 5 Minutes."*

### 4.3 Power words to keep on tap
New · Discovery · Breakthrough · Secret · Hidden · Forbidden · Banned · Revealed · Proven · Shocking · Weird · Strange · Bizarre · Little-known · Underground · Suppressed · Finally · Instantly · Overnight · Free · Guaranteed · Warning · Now · Because · You.

### 4.4 Rules
- Lead with the reader's #1 desire or #1 fear.
- Bake in **one curiosity gap** the headline does NOT resolve (the "fingerprint," the "tradition," the "switch").
- Use a number and make it odd/specific (167 lbs, 89.47%, 37%, 58%) — specifics out-pull round numbers.
- The deck should add a SECOND promise and tease the mechanism.

## 5. THE LEAD (the 350–500 words after the headline)

The lead's only job: make them unable to stop reading. Four proven types:

### 5.1 Story Lead (best for unaware/problem-aware, emotional niches)
Open on a person and a vivid moment. Build empathy, hit rock bottom, foreshadow the discovery.
- All Day Slimming Tea: Barbara, 291 lbs, fired for being "too slow," marriage dying, then collapses on a boat — *"It All Went Black…"*
- Nerve Control 911: a son watches his mother trip, then rot with foot ulcers, doctors saying "learn to live with it."
- IM-21: Tyson can't button pants 8 sizes too big; catches himself in the mirror — *"Who the hell was this fat old man?"*
- Feel Good Knees: wife cries out in agony during an intimate moment; husband feels "2 inches tall."

**Story Lead structure:** Relatable hero → normal life → inciting pain → escalation → crisis/rock-bottom → "and then I discovered…" (cliffhanger into the mechanism).

### 5.2 Mechanism / Discovery Lead (best for solution-aware, sophisticated markets)
Open on the strange new thing and its proof. *"Scientists the world over are calling the liquid in this vial 'the most consequential discovery in medicine.'"* (Cut & Paste)

### 5.3 News / Proclamation Lead (pattern-interrupt, contrarian)
*"Investing Is DEAD! Quit day trading, buying stocks, and gambling on cryptos…"* (TradeSmith). Make a bold, almost reckless claim, then spend the next lines making it credible.

### 5.4 Secret Lead
Promise a closely-guarded secret + a reason it was hidden. *"You're about to discover a hidden pain-reliever the pharmaceutical companies want to keep hidden from you."*

**Lead template (story):**
> [Sensory moment in present tense.] [Who the hero is — make the reader see themselves.] [What life used to be.] [The pain shows up, small.] [It escalates.] [The crisis scene — dialogue, tears, a doctor's verdict.] [The stakes — what they stand to lose: spouse, kids, dignity, money.] *[Cliffhanger:] "But that day turned out to be the luckiest of my life… because it led me to [the mechanism]."*

## 6. THE VILLAIN ("It's not your fault")

Every control names an enemy. This does three things: removes the reader's shame, explains why nothing worked before, and bonds you to the reader against a common foe.

Villains observed:
- **Big Pharma / drug companies** (NSAID deaths, Gabapentin "euphoric high," addictive Tramadol) — Nerve Control 911, Feel Good Knees.
- **Greedy corporations suppressing research** ("a weight-loss company spent $2.3M to bury the study") — All Day Slimming Tea.
- **Surgeons & hospitals** profiting from $50,000 knee replacements — Feel Good Knees.
- **Wall Street elites** who keep the strategy for themselves — TradeSmith.
- **The supplement industry** selling "cheap, shitty" markup product — IM-21.

**How to write the villain:**
- Make it specific and named (an industry, a practice, a dollar figure).
- Give it a motive (money, control).
- Show the human cost (deaths, addiction, bankruptcy, lost years).
- Then position your hero/product as the rebel leveling the playing field. *"I'm on a mission to level the playing field."* (TradeSmith)
- Use the magic phrase: ***"And the worst part? It's not your fault."***

## 7. THE UNIQUE MECHANISM (the heart of it all — RMBC's "M")

This is the *craft of writing* the mechanism. For Stefan's process of *finding* it out of your research, see Section 22.2.

Two mechanisms, both required:

### 7.1 The PROBLEM Mechanism — the "real hidden reason"
Reveal the surprising true cause of their problem that everyone else missed. This reframes the entire market and makes your solution the only logical answer.
- Nerve Control 911: the enzyme **MMP-13** (+ "7 Pain Pathways") — eats collagen "like walking a rough trail barefoot."
- IM-21: it's **not low testosterone** — it's your pancreas failing to digest protein, flipping you from **Growth Mode → Survival Mode**, then aromatase turning testosterone into estrogen (a "vicious cycle").
- All Day Slimming Tea: aging **mitochondria** producing less energy.
- Feel Good Knees: a **5% kneecap misalignment** causing a "4-fold increase in damage."

### 7.2 The SOLUTION Mechanism — the new lever
The unique way your product fixes the newly-revealed cause — named, trademarked-feeling, and dramatized with an analogy.
- "An herbal alliance that **handcuffs the nerve-toxin** and sends in a cleanup crew." (crime-scene analogy)
- "**Cut and paste** bad genes, paste in good ones." (software analogy — CRISPR)
- "The **1-2 Punch** that flips you back into Growth Mode." (IM-21)
- "A **time capsule in a bottle** — a blank-slate stem cell that travels to the damage." (Stem Cell)

### 7.3 How to dramatize a mechanism
1. **Name it** (caps, quotes, or a brand-y label): the "Infinite Income Loop," the "7 Pain Pathways," "Growth Mode."
2. **Analogy it** — map the invisible biology/finance to something physical: fingerprint, toll bridge, car factory turning steel into a car, software, a switch.
3. **Sequence it** — walk through step-by-step so it feels inevitable and understood.
4. **Proof it** — anchor with a study, a date, an institution.

> **Rule:** A great mechanism makes a tired promise believable again. When the market is skeptical, you don't push harder on the claim — you sharpen the mechanism.

## 8. PROOF & CREDIBILITY

Skepticism is the default. Stack proof in layers:

- **Named studies + journals + dates:** *"On March 7th, 2018 the journal Nature said…"* · *"a 2017 study took 250 people with knee osteoarthritis…"*. Specific institution + date + finding.
- **Authority figures (borrowed credibility):** Nobel committee, Bill Gates, Barron's, 60 Minutes, Mayo Clinic, named doctors/professors.
- **Hyper-specific numbers:** 89.47% win rate, 37% cartilage repair, 46,751% return, 83 lbs, $2.6 billion. *Odd, exact numbers read as true; round numbers read as made-up.*
- **Demonstrations:** TradeSmith literally walks you through placing the trade click-by-click. Show, don't just claim.
- **Founder credentials / origin story:** Dylan Jovine's 30-year Wall Street record; Coach Todd's degrees; Tyson IS the proof.
- **Social proof at scale:** "82,791 people from 135 countries," "over 10,283 men and women," "800,000 customers."

**The Specificity Rule:** every vague claim must be replaced by a concrete one. Not "lots of people," but "12,984 former nerve-pain victims." Not "made a lot," but "$18,000 richer."

## 9. EMOTION & HUMANIZED VOICE

### 9.1 The emotional arc
Most controls run: **empathy → agitation (twist the knife) → fear of the future → hope → relief → desire → urgency.** Make them feel the pain *worse* before you relieve it.

### 9.2 Agitation / twisting the knife
Don't just name the pain — make them live it:
> *"You could lay awake at 2am worrying about life after Total Knee Replacement Surgery… or being forced to use a cane or walker."* (Feel Good Knees)
> *"Even though you're thinking about buying fatter SHOES cause your damn feet keep getting bigger."* (IM-21)

### 9.3 Sensory + specific detail
Aretha Franklin on the iTunes playlist; the apple pie in the oven; pants 8 sizes too big that still won't button; "smaller pants." Concrete sensory details make fiction feel true and emotion land.

### 9.4 Future-pacing
Drop them into the after-scene: *"Imagine waking up tomorrow with as much energy as you had in your twenties…"* · *"Scooping up your 7-month-old grandbaby to nestle her in your arms."* Use "imagine," "picture," "think about how it'll feel when…"

### 9.5 Humanized, conversational diction
Write like one person talking to another, not a brand broadcasting:
- Short words. Contractions. Natural spoken rhythm. Asides in parentheses. *(Keep every line complete and flowing, never clipped — see Rule 0.1.3.)*
- Direct address ("you," "look," "listen," "here's the thing," "between you and me").
- Self-deprecation and confession build trust ("I can't believe I'm sharing this with you").

### 9.6 Voice calibration (match the avatar)
- **Raw / profane / alpha** (Tyson, male 40–60 fitness): *"Motherfucker, I'm MIKE TYSON."* Swearing, swagger, locker-room. (Only when the avatar talks this way.)
- **Authoritative / insider** (Nomi Prins, Dylan Jovine; finance): calm, credentialed, "let me show you what Wall Street won't."
- **Warm / empathetic healer** (Coach Todd, Maxwell Conrad; health): gentle, faith-tinged, "I felt your pain, here's hope."
> Decide the voice from the avatar, then keep it consistent top to bottom.

## 10. CURIOSITY, OPEN LOOPS, REVEALS & RHYTHM

### 10.1 Open loops (the engine of "keep reading")
Constantly promise a reveal you haven't delivered yet:
- *"I'll tell you all about it in just a moment, so keep reading to the very end."*
- *"First let me share my most controversial finding yet…"*
- *"But that's NOT all…"* · *"And then something happened that changed everything…"*
Never resolve every loop at once — always have one open.

### 10.2 The reveal cadence
Tease → build → partial reveal → bigger tease → full reveal at the product. The mechanism's NAME is often withheld until you've built enough desire (CRISPR isn't named for many paragraphs).

### 10.3 Rhythm & formatting (critical for web)
- **One idea per line.** One- and two-sentence "paragraphs." Tons of white space.
- **Ellipses to pull the eye down…** and create momentum.
- Vary sentence length for rhythm, but **keep every sentence complete** (Rule 0.1.3). A long, building sentence can be followed by a short one, as long as the short one feels whole and flows on, not clipped or abrupt.
- **Bold/highlight** the lines you'd want skimmers to catch (the Stefan Georgi breakdown highlights exactly the headline power words, the curiosity bullets, and the benefit lines).
- Subheads every few hundred words that each tease forward.

### 10.4 Callbacks
Plant something early, pay it off late (Barbara's empty tea jars → the whole "how do I get more" problem → the product). Closes the story loop and feels satisfying.

## 12. THE OFFER & VALUE STACK

This section is how you *write* the offer on the page. How you *build* the underlying offer (market choice, value equation, bonus engineering, guarantee types, naming) lives in **Section 21** — do that first, then present it here.

### 12.1 Name the product
Give it a brandable, benefit-or-mechanism name: "Feel Good Knees Self-Healing System," "Infinite Income Loop," "Nerve Control Emergency Response Program," "The Ultimate Anabolic Activator."

### 12.2 Stack the value
List everything they get as discrete, named, individually-valued items:
- Core product
- Components/modules (e.g., "23 HD instructional videos," "3 progressive levels")
- **Bonuses** with their own names + dollar values ("1-Minute Rejuvenation Finishers — Valued at $29.99"; Behind the Markets stacked **7 reports worth $1,497**).
- Sum it: "**Total Value: $1,696!**" — then reveal the real price far below.

### 12.3 Price anchoring + the drop ladder
Anchor against the expensive alternative, then descend:
- Feel Good Knees: PT costs $100/session → "should be $497.99" → "won't pay half, $248.99" → "$27."
- TradeSmith: "usually $5,000/year" → "you don't pay $4,000… $2,000… $1,000… $500… not even $100… just a few bucks a month."
- **Per-day reframe:** "That's like $2 a day" / "$2.33 — less than a Starbucks coffee."

### 12.4 Offer template
> Here's everything you get when you order today:
> ✅ [Core product] — ($X value)
> ✅ [Component] — ($X value)
> ✅ FREE Bonus #1: [Name] — ($X value)
> ✅ FREE Bonus #2: [Name] — ($X value)
> **Total value: $[big number].**
> But you won't pay $[anchor]… or even $[lower]…
> Today, just **$[price]** — that's about $[per-day] a day.

## 13. URGENCY & SCARCITY (ethical levers only)

Use real reasons, not fake countdown timers you can't back up:
- **Price rising:** "costs are rising, I may have to raise the price to $97 very soon." (Feel Good Knees, IM-21)
- **Limited stock / batch:** "we pre-purchased a million dollars of ingredients… this first batch will sell out." (IM-21)
- **Limited spots:** "the first 1,000 people," "3,024 people get this Cheat Sheet." (Behind the Markets, TradeSmith)
- **Window closing:** "this free video won't be available forever." (Nomi Prins)
- **Outside threat:** "I've been receiving threats from pharmaceutical lawyers… I don't know how long this site stays up." (Nerve Control 911)
- **Cost of inaction:** the most powerful — "every day you wait, cartilage deteriorates, inflammation rises, pain grows."

---

## 14. RISK REVERSAL / GUARANTEES

Make saying yes risk-free. Patterns, weakest → strongest:
- **Standard money-back:** "60-day, 100% money-back, no questions asked." (most controls)
- **Keep-the-bonuses:** "Even if you refund, the free reports are yours to keep." (Behind the Markets)
- **Better-than-free / pay-you-to-try:** "We'll refund every penny **PLUS give you an extra $100** just for giving it an honest try." (Nerve Control 911)
- **Lifetime guarantee + price lock:** "100% guaranteed, for life… and I'll lock your price forever, even if I lose money on every bag." (IM-21)
- **Reverse the burden of proof:** "You be the judge. You have to prove to yourself it works. That's why I take ALL the risk." (Feel Good Knees)

**Guarantee template:**
> Try [Product] for [30/60/90] days. If you don't [specific result], just [email/call] us and we'll refund every penny — no questions asked. [Optional: And keep the bonuses / plus we'll add $X]. That's how confident I am. The risk is 100% mine.

## 15. THE CLOSE + CTA

### 15.1 The "Two Paths" close (very effective)
Contrast the painful status-quo path vs. the transformed path:
> **Option One:** ignore it and hope it goes away — while inside your knee, cartilage deteriorates and pain grows.
> **Option Two:** the natural self-healing method that brings your knee back to life. (Feel Good Knees)

### 15.2 CTA rules
- Tell them *exactly* what happens next: "Click the button below, you'll be taken to a secure page, put in your details…"
- Repeat the CTA multiple times through the offer/close (every screen of scrolling on web).
- Use a button-label that restates value: "CLAIM YOUR COPY WITH FREE BONUSES NOW," "Redeem Special Offer," "Get Started."
- End on the dream + a warm sign-off: "I hope to see you inside."

### 15.3 The PS (1–3 of them) — many readers jump straight here
- **PS #1 — restate the core promise/idea + urgency:** "Remember, I'm not sure how long I can hold this price."
- **PS #2 — re-assert the guarantee / remove risk.**
- **PS #3 — cost of inaction / final emotional nudge.**

## 16. OBJECTION HANDLING / FAQ

Anticipate and disarm every "yeah, but…":
- *"Is this for me even if I've tried everything / had surgery / am old?"* → "This is exactly who I made it for." (Feel Good Knees, IM-21)
- *"Sounds too good to be true."* → name the skepticism out loud, then over-prove. ("I know it sounds crazy… but you can see the proof.")
- *"How fast / how do I take it?"* → make it effortless ("rip, pour, stir, drink"; "5 minutes a day from your recliner").
- *"Is it safe / real?"* → GMP, third-party tested, "Is this really Mike Tyson? Yes."
Put the spikiest objections in a FAQ block near the order section.

## 17. COMPLIANCE AWARENESS (write persuasive AND defensible)

Not legal advice — but these controls all hedge, and so should you:
- **Testimonial disclaimer:** "Results are not typical / don't represent the average purchaser." (Feel Good Knees, TradeSmith)
- **Income/health caveats:** "Investing carries risk; you may lose your investment." · "Consult your doctor before starting."
- Avoid stating or implying a **cure** for a disease in regulated niches; use "support," "help," "may," "designed to."
- Keep claims tied to the cited studies; don't overstate beyond the proof.
- Names/photos may be changed "to protect privacy" — disclosed in the legal footer.
> Persuasion lives in the story and mechanism; let the claims stay inside what you can defend.

## 21. THE GRAND SLAM OFFER ENGINE — build the offer before you write a word

*Distilled, chapter by chapter, from Alex Hormozi's $100M Offers: How to Make Offers So Good People Feel Stupid Saying No.*

Sections 1–20 teach you to *sell* the offer. This section teaches you to *build* one worth selling. **Run this FIRST.** The best copy in the world cannot save a weak offer; a Grand Slam Offer is so good that mediocre copy still converts. Do this, then carry the result into the Brief (1) and the offer-copy sections (12–14).

### 21.1 What a Grand Slam Offer is
An offer so good people feel stupid saying no — and so differentiated it can't be price-compared to anything else in the market. It combines five things into one irresistible package:
- An **attractive promotion** (how they hear about it)
- An **unmatchable value proposition** (the Value Equation, 21.5)
- A **premium price** (21.4)
- An **unbeatable guarantee** (21.10)
- A **money model / payment terms** that removes the cash barrier

The job of the offer: change the question in the prospect's head from *"Should I buy this?"* to *"How could I possibly NOT buy this?"* When you sell a true category-of-one, you escape price comparison entirely.

### 21.2 Escape the commodity trap
A commodity is something available elsewhere — so buyers decide on price, and you bleed in a race to the bottom. The only escape is to become **incomparable.** Don't compete on being cheaper; win by being the *only* thing that does *this*, *this way*, for *this person*. (This is the offer-level twin of the Unique Mechanism, Section 7 — the mechanism is what makes the offer un-compareable.) **Rule: differentiate or die.**

### 21.3 Step 1 — Pick a Starving Crowd (market beats everything)
"A starving crowd" — the single most important choice. A great offer to a bad market loses; even an average offer to a desperate, well-funded market wins.

Score any market on four traits (all four should be strong):
- **Massive Pain** — they hurt enough to act now (the more painful, the more they pay).
- **Purchasing Power** — they can actually afford it.
- **Easy to Target** — reachable lists, groups, audiences, channels.
- **Growing** — a market with a tailwind, not a dying one.

Then **commit and niche down** — "riches are in the niches." The progression: broad market → narrower → specific avatar. A specific offer ("for [exact avatar] who want [exact result]") out-prices and out-converts a generic one, because specificity raises perceived likelihood of success. Pick one and go deep; don't market-hop.

### 21.4 Step 2 — Charge a Premium (the price↔value relationship)
You sell inside the **price-to-value discrepancy**: the bigger the gap between perceived value and price, the easier the yes. The lever is to **raise value, not cut price.**
- **The Virtuous Cycle of Price:** raise price → client has more skin in the game → they're more emotionally invested and act → they get better results → better testimonials & referrals → you can charge even more. (Discounting runs this cycle in reverse.)
- **Higher price increases perceived value** — price is itself a signal (the wine/placebo effect). Cheap reads as low-quality.
- Don't be "more for less." Be premium. Pick a price that makes you slightly uncomfortable.
- This is why the playbook anchors high and never leads with "cheap" (see 12.3) — the per-day reframe *justifies* a premium, it doesn't apologize for it.

### 21.5 Step 3 — The Value Equation (the master formula)
Everything that makes an offer valuable reduces to four variables:

> **Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

Drive the top two **UP** and the bottom two toward **ZERO** — as the denominator approaches zero, perceived value approaches infinite.

| Lever | Push | What it means | Where it shows up in the copy |
|-------|------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| **Dream Outcome** | ↑ max | The specific life they crave | 2.5 (sell the destination), 8 (future-pacing) |
| **Perceived Likelihood** | ↑ max | Believable proof it'll work for them | 7 (mechanism), 8 (proof), 9 (testimonials), 14 (guarantee) |
| **Time Delay** | ↓ to ~0 | How fast they get the result | "in 5 minutes," "overnight," "while you sleep" |
| **Effort & Sacrifice** | ↓ to ~0 | How little they must do/give up | "WITHOUT diet or exercise," "rip, pour, drink" |

Use it as a **scorecard**: rate any offer or claim 1–10 on each lever; the weakest lever is your next thing to fix. Most "lose weight" offers die on Effort and Time — which is exactly why "melt fat WITHOUT diet or exercise, while you sleep" wins.

### 21.6 Step 4 — The Offer-Creation Process (the actual build)
This is the workshop that produces your stack:
1. **Define the Dream Outcome** — write the end state in the prospect's own words.
2. **List EVERY Problem** — brainstorm every obstacle they hit before, during, and after getting the result. Be exhaustive and brutal; each problem is a future objection. (Run each problem through the four Value-Equation dimensions — what makes it slow, hard, unlikely, or far from the dream.)
3. **Turn each Problem into a Solution** — invert every problem into a "how to ___" deliverable. Your problem list is your solution list, flipped.
4. **Trim & Stack (the Delivery Cube)** — for each solution decide *how* to deliver it, then keep only the winners:
   - **Delivery dimensions to vary:** group vs. 1-on-1 · done-for-you vs. done-with-you vs. do-it-yourself · level & speed of support/response · format (live, recorded, doc, software, physical, template/checklist) · effort required of them · who delivers it (and their credentials).
   - **The 10x / ¹⁄₁₀ test:** "If a client paid me 10× the price, what would I add?" and "If I could only charge ¹⁄₁₀, what would I be forced to do?" — both surface high-value moves.
   - **Evaluate on a value-vs-cost grid:** cut high-cost / low-value; keep low-cost / high-value (margin) AND high-cost / high-value (your differentiators). Drop everything else.
5. The surviving solutions become your **named, stacked components & bonuses** (presented in 12.2).

### 21.7 Enhance — Scarcity (limited supply)
Limiting supply raises perceived value and forces a decision. Honest forms:
- Limited units / seats / slots (per cohort or total).
- Limited bonuses (only the first N get the extra).
- Never-again availability ("this offer won't return").

(Ties directly to Section 13 — use real limits only, never fake counters.)

### 21.8 Enhance — Urgency (limited time)
A deadline converts "someday" into "now." Four reusable engines:
- **Cohort-based** — "doors close Friday, next class starts in 8 weeks."
- **Rolling seasonal** — recurring real deadlines (end of month/quarter/promo).
- **Pricing / bonus deadline** — price rises or a bonus disappears at a date.
- **Exploding opportunity** — the chance itself genuinely expires.

Always pair urgency with **cost of inaction** (13).

### 21.9 Enhance — Bonuses (the stack multiplier)
The same total value, broken into separately named and priced bonuses, out-converts one lumped offer. Rules:
- **Ask for the sale FIRST**, then pile on the bonuses (each one tips the fence-sitter further).
- Each bonus kills a **specific problem/objection** from your Step-2 list.
- **Name** every bonus with a benefit-driven headline, and assign it a **dollar value**.
- Prefer **tools, templates, checklists, and shortcuts** over more information — they cut Time & Effort (good for the Value Equation).
- Add scarcity to a bonus to sharpen it.
- Use other companies' products (partner/affiliate deals) as bonuses — value to you is free.
- Stack the total value far above the price, so the gap (21.4) is obvious. (Feeds 12.2 directly.)

### 21.10 Enhance — Guarantees (the single biggest conversion lever)
A guarantee reverses risk and is, dollar-for-dollar, the highest-leverage element you can add. Four types (mix and stack):
- **Unconditional** — strongest. "No questions asked, full refund." Maximum trust, maximum exposure.
- **Conditional** — "better than money-back," with terms/hoops the buyer must meet. Includes service guarantees ("we work for free until you hit X") and outsized guarantees ("we refund AND pay you $___"). (Nerve Control 911's "refund + extra $100" lives here — see 14.)
- **Anti-Guarantee** — "all sales final," with a stated reason why (exclusivity, high-ticket, "this isn't for the uncommitted"). Used to attract serious buyers.
- **Implied / Performance** — "we only win when you win": revenue-share, profit-share, pay-on-results.

Make the guarantee specific to their #1 desired result and #1 fear, give it a name, and consider stacking two (e.g., unconditional + outsized). (Feeds Section 14.)

### 21.11 Name — the M-A-G-I-C Formula
A flat product/offer name kills response; a magnetic one earns the click. Build the name from these pieces (use 3–5, not all):
- **M — Magnetic "Reason Why":** Free, $1, Giveaway, Spring, Last-Chance, Charity…
- **A — Announce the Avatar:** exactly who it's for ("for busy moms," "for nerve-pain sufferers").
- **G — Goal / dream outcome:** the result ("melt fat," "kill knee pain").
- **I — Indication of Time interval:** how long to the result ("in 21 days," "5-minute").
- **C — Container word:** System, Blueprint, Bootcamp, Masterclass, Challenge, Toolkit, Program, Method, Protocol, Formula.

Example shape: "The [Avatar] [Goal] [Time] [Container]" → "The Busy-Mom 21-Day Fat-Melt Challenge." Rotate / rename when response fatigues — a fresh name on the same offer beats ad burnout. (Feeds 12.1 product naming and Section 4 headlines.)

### 21.12 How the Engine plugs into the Playbook
| Build (Section 21) | → flows into → | Write (Sections 1–20) |
|--------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Starving crowd (21.3) | → | Avatar + Market Awareness/Sophistication (1, 2.3–2.4) |
| Category-of-one / incomparability (21.2) | → | Big Idea + Unique Mechanism (2.1, 7) |
| Value Equation (21.5) | → | Destination + future-pacing + proof (2.5, 8) |
| Offer-creation stack (21.6, 21.9) | → | Offer & Value Stack copy (12) |
| Premium pricing (21.4) | → | Price anchor + per-day drop (12.3) |
| Guarantees (21.10) | → | Risk Reversal (14) |
| Scarcity + Urgency (21.7–21.8) | → | Urgency/Scarcity copy (13) |
| M-A-G-I-C name (21.11) | → | Product name + headline (12.1, 4) |

**One-line summary:** Market → Value Equation → list problems → flip to solutions → trim & stack → enhance with scarcity, urgency, bonuses, and a guarantee → name it with MAGIC. That's a Grand Slam Offer. *Then* — and only then — write the copy.

## 22. THE RMBC METHOD — THE MASTER WRITING SYSTEM

*Stefan Georgi's RMBC = Research → Mechanism → Brief → Copy.* This is the operating system the entire playbook runs on (see Section 0). Everything else in this file is a tool you reach for inside one of these four phases. **Execute in order, every time. No skipping.**

### 22.0 Why RMBC works
Most copy fails because the writer started typing before they knew anything — so they wrote vague, me-too, "feature" copy from their own head. RMBC flips it: you do the **knowing** first (Research), find the **angle** (Mechanism), make every **decision** on paper (Brief), and only then **write** (Copy). Done right, roughly half your time is spent *before* you write a single sentence of prose — and the copy then "writes itself." Research is the unfair advantage; the better your research, the easier and better the copy. **The order is sacred: R → M → B → C.**

### 22.1 R — RESEARCH (spend ~50% of total time here)
The goal of Research is to end up with a **Research Doc** so full of the avatar's exact words, real pains, objections, dreams, and hard proof that writing becomes fill-in-the-blanks. Research four targets:

**22.1.1 Product Research — know what you're selling cold**
Extract and write down:
- Every ingredient / component / module — and what each one *does* (the benefit, not just the feature).
- Dosages, sourcing, certifications (GMP, third-party tested, organic, patents).
- How it works mechanically — the raw material for your Mechanism.
- What makes it different from everything else on the shelf.
- Origin story + founder credentials (borrowed authority, Section 8).
- Format / ease of use ("rip, pour, drink"; "5 minutes from your recliner").
- The full offer: price, bonuses, guarantee, shipping, upsells.

*Sources:* the product page, the supplement-facts/spec label, the founder's bio, any existing sales material.

**22.1.2 Avatar Research — Voice of Customer (the most important step)**
The #1 rule of RMBC research: **don't invent the avatar's language — steal it.** The exact words real buyers use ARE your headline, your bullets, and your objections. This is "Voice of Customer" (VOC) mining.

*Where to mine* (go where your avatar complains and celebrates):
- Amazon reviews of competing products — the goldmine. Read dozens.
- Reddit (niche subreddits), Quora, niche forums, Facebook groups.
- YouTube comments on related videos, blog comments, app-store reviews.
- Competitor testimonials and their own sales pages.

*The 5★ / 1★ split* (do both):
- **5★ reviews → desire.** What they LOVE, the dream outcome in their words, the transformation, the "I finally…" moments. → fuels your Promise, Benefits (Section 8), and future-pacing.
- **1★ reviews → objections & fears.** What made them distrust, what went wrong, what they were skeptical about, the deal-breakers. → fuels your Objection-handling (Section 16), Villain (Section 6), and Guarantee (Section 14).

*What to extract and paste verbatim into the Research Doc:*
- The surface pain and the deep/emotional pain (shame, fear, identity, letting loved ones down).
- The dream / desired end-state — in their exact phrasing.
- Every objection and skepticism ("tried everything," "too good to be true," "too expensive," "I'm too old").
- Failed past solutions — what they've already wasted money on (sets up "it's not your fault").
- Power phrases — the vivid, emotional, oddly specific things only a real sufferer says. These become headlines and leads.

**22.1.3 Market / Competitor Research — know the conversation**
- What ads, VSLs, and funnels are already running (winners have been running a long time — that's proof of a working angle).
- What claims the market is making → tells you the **Sophistication** level (2.4): are buyers hearing claim #1 or claim #500?
- What mechanisms competitors use (so yours is different, not a copy).
- Their offers, prices, guarantees, bonuses (so you can out-offer them — Section 21).
- The gap: what is nobody saying? That white space is your angle.

*Sources:* Facebook Ad Library, ClickBank / affiliate marketplaces, competitor landers & VSLs, Google/YouTube searches for the product category.

**22.1.4 Proof / Science Research — earn the right to claim**
- Find studies supporting the ingredients, the mechanism, and the claims.
- Pull specific stats, institutions, dates, and authorities (the more specific, the more credible — Section 8).
- *Sources:* PubMed, Google Scholar, examine.com, study citations on competitor pages, the product's own references.
- **Rule:** never make a claim the research can't defend (Section 17). Tie every claim back to a source.

**22.1.5 The Research Doc — questions every project must answer**
Organize raw findings under these headers (copy/paste quotes liberally):
1. What exactly is the product, and how does it work?
2. Who is the ONE avatar? (demographics + psychographics + a day in their life)
3. What is their deepest pain — surface and emotional?
4. What is their biggest desire / dream outcome?
5. What have they already tried that failed, and why?
6. What do they secretly believe / fear / distrust? (objections)
7. What is the hidden root cause of their problem? (→ Mechanism)
8. How does the product uniquely fix that cause? (→ Mechanism)
9. What proof exists? (studies, stats, authorities, testimonials)
10. What is the market's sophistication & awareness level?
11. What are competitors claiming — and what's the unsaid gap?
12. What's the offer (price, bonuses, guarantee)?

If you can answer these twelve from real evidence, the Mechanism, Brief, and Copy fall out almost automatically.

### 22.2 M — MECHANISM (turn research into the "why")
The Mechanism is what makes a tired promise believable again — the engine of the whole letter. Two are required (full writing craft in Section 7):
- **Unique Mechanism of the PROBLEM** — the surprising, hidden root cause the market didn't know about. It reframes everything and explains why nothing else worked ("it's not your fault — the real reason is ___").
- **Unique Mechanism of the SOLUTION** — the unique lever your product pulls to fix that exact cause, that nothing else does.

**Stefan's test for a strong mechanism** — it must be all three:
- **Plausible / science-backed** — anchored to something in your Proof research (22.1.4).
- **Novel to this market** — not the claim they've heard 500 times (matches Sophistication, 2.4).
- **Owned by the product** — the product must credibly address it, or the mechanism is useless.

Then name it, simplify it, and analogize it (Section 7.3) so a distracted reader "gets it" instantly. When the market is skeptical, you don't push harder on the claim — you sharpen the mechanism.

### 22.3 B — THE BRIEF (decide everything before you write)
The Brief is the bridge: it converts the Research Doc + Mechanism into a single battle plan so the writing is mechanical. Make every strategic decision here — if a decision isn't in the brief, you'll waffle in the draft.

### 22.4 C — THE COPY (write fast, edit slow)
With R, M, and B done, copy is largely assembly: pour the brief into the Skeleton (Section 3). Stefan's writing rules:
- **The Lead is everything.** Spend a disproportionate share of effort on the first 10–15% — if the lead doesn't hook, nothing after it matters. (Lead types & templates: Section 5.)
- **Write a "vomit draft."** Draft the whole thing fast and badly, start to finish, without editing as you go. Momentum beats perfection; you cannot write and judge at the same time. Get the clay on the table first.
- **One idea per sentence.** Talk, don't write. Contractions, short words, direct address — like one human to one human (Section 9.5).
- **Keep a loop open at all times.** Bucket brigades and open loops pull the eye down the page (Section 10).
- **Then edit in separate passes** (never all at once):
  - **Pass 1 — Structure/flow:** is every section doing its job, in order, with teasing transitions?
  - **Pass 2 — Cut:** delete anything not advancing the story, deepening emotion, proving a claim, or opening/closing curiosity.
  - **Pass 3 — Punch:** replace every vague word with a specific number/name/sensory detail; sharpen the emotion.
  - **Pass 4 — Read it ALOUD:** fix anything you stumble on; copy must sound like speech.
  - **Pass 5 — Proof & compliance:** every claim tied to a source; hedges in place (Section 17).
- Run the **"Gun-to-the-Head" QA** (Section 20) and rewrite every "no."
